Related Work:
In high-impact journals, the Related Work (or Literature Review) section typically spans 1 to 1.5 pages. Its primary goal is not just to list previous studies, but to build a narrative funnel that proves your research is novel and necessary.

The Related Work Flow (The "Funnel" Model)
High-impact authors organize this section by themes or methodologies rather than paper-by-paper, following this logical progression: 

Broad Thematic Grouping: Start by identifying 2–3 major "schools of thought" or technical approaches that have addressed your problem. Group similar papers together to show you understand the overall landscape.

The Critical Comparison: For each group, summarize the key technical contributions but—more importantly—identify their limitations or boundary conditions.

The "Closer-to-Home" Works: Dedicate more detail (1–3 sentences) to papers that are technically closest to your own.
The Synthesis & Gap (The Exit): End the section by explicitly stating how your work differs from all the above. Use clear contrastive phrases like "In contrast to Method X, our approach..." to highlight your novelty.

System Architecture/System Design/System Model:
In high-impact systems papers (e.g., IEEE Transactions, ACM SOSP/OSDI, or Nature Electronics), the System Architecture section is the "intellectual meat" of the paper. It moves from high-level concepts to granular technical logic.
1. The Section Flow (The "Logic of Construction")
A professional architecture section usually follows this 4-step internal flow:
Design Principles/Goals: Don't just say what you built; explain why. List 3–4 high-level goals (e.g., scalability, low latency, fault tolerance) that guided your architectural choices.
High-Level Overview: Introduce a "Big Picture" diagram. Briefly describe the main modules (e.g., Data Ingestion Layer, Processing Engine, Storage) and how they interact.
Component-Level Detail: Break the system down. Use sub-sections for each major module. Explain the unique algorithms, data structures, or hardware configurations used in each.
Data/Control Flow: Explain the "life of a request" or how data moves through the system from input to output. This clarifies the operational logic.
2. Physical Length & Word Count
This is typically the most dense section of the paper.
Metric	Standard Range
Physical Length	2 to 4 pages (including diagrams)
Proportion	25% – 35% of the entire manuscript

Unlike the Introduction or Related Work, the Architecture section is about your original work, so the reference count drops significantly here.
Count: 3 to 8 references.
Usage: Only cite external works when mentioning a specific library, a baseline protocol you modified, or a standard hardware component (e.g., "We utilized the Xilinx Vivado suite [12] for synthesis").
4. Key Facts for High-Impact Success
The "Golden Diagram": High-impact journals almost always require one high-quality, professional vector diagram (SVG/EPS) that summarizes the entire architecture. If this diagram is confusing, the paper is often rejected.
Justification Over Description: Low-tier papers just describe the system. High-impact papers justify it (e.g., "We chose a decentralized DHT over a central server to eliminate the single point of failure identified in [Reference X]").
Formalism: If applicable, include a small table of notations or a brief mathematical model defining the system's state. This adds the "rigour" expected by top-tier reviewers.



PROPOSED METHODOLOGY or IMPLEMENTATION:
The PROPOSED METHODOLOGY or Implementation section in high-impact systems or engineering journals, this section is the "proof of feasibility." While Architecture (Section 3) explains the blueprint, Implementation/Methodology (Section 4) explains the construction.
1. The Section Flow (The "Translation" Logic)
This section should move from the abstract design to the concrete realization:
Implementation Environment: Define the "stack." Specify the hardware (CPUs, GPUs, FPGA models), operating systems (Linux kernel versions), and primary languages/frameworks (Python, C++, TensorFlow or any) used.
Key Algorithms/Pseudo-code: If your methodology involves a specific process or logic, present it here. High-impact journals prefer elegant pseudo-code (10–15 lines) over raw code snippets.
The "Secret Sauce" (Optimization): Detail the non-obvious engineering choices made to realize the architecture. Did you use custom memory management? A specific concurrency model? This proves technical depth.
Data Pre-processing/Integration: Describe how data was ingested, cleaned, or transformed before entering the system.
Prototype Scope: Quantify the effort. Mention the total Lines of Code (LoC) or the number of modules developed to show the study's scale.
2. Physical Length & Word Count
This section is usually leaner than the Architecture but denser than the Introduction.
Metric	Standard Range
Physical Length	1 to 2 pages
Word Count	800 – 1,200 words
Proportion	15% – 20% of the manuscript
3. Reference Count
References here are functional rather than theoretical.
Count: 5 to 10 references.
Usage: Cite specific libraries (e.g., NumPy, OpenCV), APIs, compilers, or open-source repositories that your implementation builds upon.
4. Key Facts for High-Impact Success
Reproducibility is King: Reviewers at top journals (like Nature or IEEE) will look for enough detail to replicate your work. If you used a specific version of a library that has known bugs, mention it.
The "How" vs. the "Why": While Section 3 explained why a component exists, Section 4 must explain how it was built. Use phrases like "To achieve the low latency described in Section 3, we implemented..."
Hardware/Software Specifics: Be precise. Instead of "a high-end GPU," say "for example Lenovo Ryzen 9 7945HX with NVIDIA RTX 4060 GPU (8 GB) with and 16 GB RAM." but you must first verify my system details and then enter the real system configurations.
Artifact Availability: High-impact papers often include a "Data/Code Availability" statement here. Mentioning that your code is on GitHub or Zenodo significantly boosts your impact score.



Evaluation and Results:
In high-impact journals, the Evaluation and Results section is the most critical part of your "pitch." It proves that your system actually works and outperforms the state-of-the-art. In top-tier venues (e.g., Nature, IEEE, ACM), this is often the longest section.
1. The Section Flow (The "Validation" Logic)
A high-impact evaluation doesn't just list numbers; it tells a story of performance through four distinct stages:
Experimental Setup: Briefly describe the environment (testbed, datasets, or workloads). Define your Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)—e.g., latency, throughput, energy efficiency, or accuracy.
Baselines for Comparison: Explicitly state which existing systems (State-of-the-Art) you are comparing against. This is vital for showing superiority.
Main Results (The "Headlines"): Start with your strongest finding. Use clear, multi-panel figures to show how your system performs under standard conditions compared to baselines.
Ablation Study / Sensitivity Analysis: This is the "high-impact" secret. Break your system apart to show which specific component contributes most to the success. (e.g., "Performance drops by 30% without the custom cache described in Section 3.")
Discussion of Trade-offs: Acknowledge where the system might struggle (e.g., "Higher accuracy comes at the cost of 5% more power consumption"). This builds scientific credibility.
2. Physical Length & Word Count
This section must be data-heavy and visually driven.
Metric	Standard Range
Physical Length	3 to 5 pages (including 4–6 major figures/tables)
Word Count	1,500 – 2,500 words
Proportion	30% – 40% of the manuscript
3. Reference Count
References here are used to cite the "Baselines" or datasets you are using.
Count: 5 to 12 references.
Usage: Cite the original papers of the algorithms/systems you are beating and the standard datasets (e.g., ImageNet, SPEC benchmarks) you used.
4. Key Facts for High-Impact Success
Lead with Visuals: In high-impact journals, the text often serves to "narrate" the figures. Ensure your figure captions are self-explanatory.
Statistical Significance: Don't just report averages. Include error bars, p-values, or confidence intervals. High-impact reviewers will reject "bare" numbers.
The "Why" of the Results: Don't just say "System A is faster." Explain why based on your architecture (e.g., "The 20% speedup is attributed to the parallel processing logic detailed in Section 3.2").
Scalability: High-impact journals love to see how a system behaves as the load increases (e.g., "Stress Testing").


Conclusion:
The Conclusion of a high-impact journal is not just a summary; it is your "final pitch" to the reader and the editor about the lasting significance of your work. It should shift from the specific data of your results to the global impact of your findings.
1. The Section Flow (The "Exit Strategy")
A high-impact conclusion usually follows a 4-step "reverse funnel" flow:
The Main Takeaway (The "What"): Re-state the primary problem you addressed and the core solution/finding. Do not simply copy-paste your abstract; rephrase it to emphasize the success of the system.
Significance & Contribution (The "So What?"): Explain how your results change the field. Does your architecture enable a new type of application? Does it disprove a previous assumption? This is where you justify the "High Impact" label.
Limitations & Boundaries: Be honest. Briefly mention the conditions under which your system might not perform or what you haven't solved yet. This builds academic maturity and forestalls reviewer criticism.
Future Work & Vision: End on a forward-looking note. Suggest the next logical steps for the research community. High-impact papers often conclude with a visionary statement about where this technology could lead in 5–10 years.
2. Physical Length & Word Count
The conclusion should be "short and punchy." Long, rambling conclusions dilute the impact.
Metric	Standard Range
Physical Length	0.5 to 0.75 pages (seldom a full page)
Word Count	300 – 600 words
Proportion	5% – 10% of the manuscript
3. Reference Count
References are generally avoided in the conclusion.
Count: 0 to 2 references.
Usage: If you must cite, it’s usually to a "landmark" paper you’ve finally surpassed or a high-level policy/theory your work supports.
4. Key Facts for High-Impact Success
No New Data: Never introduce a new graph, table, or result in the conclusion. If it’s important, it belongs in Section 5.
The "Broad Audience" Appeal: While the middle of your paper was for experts, the conclusion should be readable by a generalist scientist. Use clearer, less jargon-heavy language.
Actionable Insights: High-impact journals love conclusions that provide "design patterns" or "guidelines" that other researchers can immediately apply to their own systems.
Avoid Weak Language: Don't say "We hope this helps." Use assertive language like "Our framework provides a scalable solution for..." or "These results establish a new baseline for..."



Limitations and Future Work section:
In high-impact journals (like Computer Networks or IEEE Transactions), the Limitations and Future Work section is often the "reviewer-proofing" part of the paper. By identifying your own weaknesses, you prevent reviewers from using them as reasons for rejection. 
In many top-tier journals, this is either a standalone section (Section 6) or the first half of the Conclusion.
1. The Section Flow (The "Honest Scientist" Logic)
This section follows a "Problem-Solution-Vision" flow:
The Bound Check (Internal Limitations): Start by defining the scope. Where does your system not work? (e.g., "Our protocol is optimized for static topologies and may show increased latency in highly mobile ad-hoc networks.")
The Scaling Gap (External Limitations): Address constraints related to the environment, such as dataset size, hardware costs, or specific energy requirements.
The Immediate "Next Step": Propose a concrete technical improvement that builds directly on your current implementation (e.g., "Future work will focus on integrating AI-driven congestion control to replace the static threshold used in Section 4.")
The Long-Term Vision: Expand to the broader field. How could your methodology be adapted for a different domain? (e.g., "Beyond 5G, this architectural pattern could be applied to underwater acoustic networks.") 
2. Physical Length & Word Count
This should be concise. If it’s too long, it makes your current work look incomplete; if it’s too short, it looks like you haven't thought deeply about the field.
Metric	Standard Range
Physical Length	0.5 to 1 page
Word Count	400 – 800 words
Proportion	5% – 10% of the manuscript
3. Reference Count
Count: 2 to 5 references.
Usage: Cite recent "cutting-edge" papers or emerging standards (e.g., 6G whitepapers or new RFCs) to show that your future work is aligned with the direction of the industry.
4. Key Facts for High-Impact Success
Constructive Self-Criticism: High-impact reviewers respect authors who acknowledge "edge cases." It shows the work is ready for real-world deployment, not just a lab "toy."
The "Defensive" Move: If you know your dataset was small, mention it here as a limitation before the reviewer points it out. Then, explain how the current results are still statistically significant.
Avoid "Vague-speak": Don't say "We will do more tests." Say "We plan to evaluate the system under a 1,000-node simulation to stress-test the control plane overhead."
Intellectual Generosity: Providing a clear "Future Work" path helps other researchers cite you when they start their own projects based on your "suggestions."
Pro-Tip for Computer Networks: Elsevier journals often prefer this to be a subsection within the Discussion or Conclusion. Using the heading "Discussion and Future Work" is a common way to merge the two smoothly.

How do I compare and contrast two things in my paper?
You can compare and contrast in different ways. The first is to bridge two sentences with a connector that shows how two things are similar or different.  For example: ‘Smith used approach A. In contrast, Berkmans used approach B. ‘

These are the most common compare/contrast connectors used in research writing:

To compare: Similarly, Likewise, In the same way, In a similar way,
To contrast: However, On the other hand, In contrast, Nevertheless, On the contrary, Nonetheless, Instead, Yet, Despite this, Rather,
You can also mention the linking word within a sentence, instead of at the start of a sentence. With the phrase ‘in contrast’, this could look like this: **‘Smith used approach A. Berkmans, in contrast, used approach B.’

Besides using connectors, you can use one of the following sentence structures to indicate a comparison or contrast:

To compare:

Smith and Berkmans both used approach A.
Smith, like Berkmans, used approach A.
Approach A, used by Smith, was also used by Berkmans.
Approach A was used by (both) Smith and Berkmans.
The same approach was used by Smith and Berkmans, namely, approach A.
To contrast:

Smith used approach A; however, Berkmans used approach B.

Smith and Berkmans used approach A and B, respectively.

Should I use ‘need not’ or ‘does not need to’ in academic writing?

Should I use ‘has got’ or ‘has gotten’ in academic writing?
Which one you should use depends on the English variety you’re following: UK or US English.

‘Has got’ is the present perfect tense of the verb ‘to get’ in UK English. For example: ‘The subject gets an invitation.’ > ‘The subject got an invitation.’ > ‘The subject has got an invitation.’

‘Has gotten’ is the present perfect tense of the verb ‘to get’ in US English: ‘The subject has gotten an invitation’.

In US English, ‘has got’ is used too, but in ways that are too informal for academic writing, namely:

as the present tense of ‘to have’. An example would be ‘This study has got several limitations’, but in an academic text, you would write ‘This study has several limitations’.
to mean ‘must’; for example, ‘This value has got to be multiplied.’ In an academic text, it would be more appropriate to write: ‘This value must be multiplied.’

How should I use ‘either … or’ in academic writing?
You can use ‘either/or’ in academic writing to emphasize that one of two options can apply. In informal language, especially in speech, ‘either/or’ is sometimes used with more than two options too; for example, ‘Participants could either swim, run, or walk.’ However, strictly speaking, ‘either/or’ is used when there are two options only, so for academic writing, it is best to stick to this too.

This means that you can write:

‘Participants could swim or run’ (they could do one of the two, possibly both - this isn’t clear), or
‘Participants could either swim or run’ (they could do only one of the two - if one applies, the other does not)
To keep your ‘either/or’ easy to read, keep them close to each other and to the elements they refer to. For example, instead of writing ‘either applying A or B’, write ‘applying either A or B’.

How should I use ‘both … and’ in academic writing?
‘Both/and’ can be used to emphasize that two things or people apply. If you don’t want this emphasis, use ‘and’ only. ‘Both/and’ is fine to use in academic writing; it is not informal, nor is it very formal.

Compare:

Students completed an immediate and delayed post-test.
Students completed both an immediate and delayed post-test.
The second sentence emphasizes that all students took both tests.

‘Both/and’ is always followed by a plural verb form, even if the two elements are singular. For example, ‘Both the immediate and delayed post-test cover (and not ‘covers’) 20 questions.’.

To keep your ‘both/and’ clear, make sure there isn’t too much distance between the two, nor between the elements they refer to. For instance, instead of writing ‘both the peaks of A and B’, write ‘the peaks of both A and B.’

Can I use expressions like ‘of course’ and ‘needless to say’ in academic writing?
Whether or not such expressions are acceptable depends on the discipline, the type of publication, and most of all, the reader. In general, the arts and humanities and some social sciences allow more space for such expressions than the natural sciences do. In terms of publication types, you are more likely to find them being used in letters to the editor or book reviews than in research articles.

In research articles, you want to be objective and neutral. Using expressions like ‘of course’ and ‘needless to say’ are not in line with this, as they suggest your findings or interpretations are obvious - and maybe they aren’t to the reader.

Consider removing ‘needless to say’ and ‘of course’. Here are some alternatives to the sentence ‘Needless to say, eating fruit is healthier than eating chocolate.’:

It is well-known that eating fruit is healthier than eating chocolate.
As (widely) known, eating fruit is healthier than eating chocolate.
Eating fruit is healthier than eating chocolate.
Many studies [add references] have shown that eating fruit is healthier than eating chocolate.

What should I use in academic writing: ‘regarding’, ‘with regard to’, ‘with regards to’, ‘in regards with’, or ‘in regards to’?
First, in any type of writing, avoid the phrases ‘with regards to’, ‘in regards with’, and ‘in regards to’. Some of those are used, but they are incorrect. Instead, use ‘in regard to’ or, more commonly, ‘with regard to’.

You can also use ‘regarding’; if the above forms are confusing, it might be safe to stick to that instead.

All forms mean ‘concerning’ or ‘about’.

For example:

This study explores physician attitudes in regard to patient interaction.
This study explores physician attitudes with regard to patient interaction.
This study explores physician attitudes regarding patient interaction.
This study explores physician attitudes concerning patient interaction.

Should I write ‘the table/figure below’ or ‘the below table/figure’?
While you sometimes see the words ‘below’ or ‘above’ positioned after the noun, the correct way is to place them before it: ‘the table below’ or ‘the figure below’. This is because the word ‘below’ does not describe the table or figure; it only states where they are positioned (just like you would say ‘the house there’ and not ‘the there house’).

Another tip: For clarity, it’s best not avoid words and phrases like ‘above’, ‘below’, ‘here’, ‘next’, ‘in the next section’, ‘in the paragraph above’ or ‘on the previous page’. This is because online publications lack the spatial properties that physical journals and books have. The text, tables, and figures can be positioned anywhere once published - and what is above or below in your document may be elsewhere for the reader. So instead, refer to the table or figure number or, where relevant, to the section number. For example, instead of ‘the table below shows’, write ‘Table 5 shows’, and instead of ‘the next section’, write ‘Section 3.1’.

Should I write ‘data is’ or ‘data are’ in academic writing?
In most cases, it’s best to use ‘data are’. And here’s why.

The word ‘data’ is often used as a mass noun, like ‘information’ or ‘research’. Similarly to these, it is thought as an uncountable whole rather than a combination of multiple elements. For this reason, ‘data is’ is more common than ‘data are’, especially in spoken language.

However, many people consider ‘data is’ incorrect. This comes from the fact that in Latin, ‘data’ is the plural form of the word ‘datum’; and based on this, ‘data’ should be followed by a plural verb form (‘data are’).

In academic writing, where language accuracy is key, it is safest to go with ‘data are’, and follow the Latin-derived grammar rule.

A few examples:

The survey response data were collected in May, 2021.
The data in Table 4 show an increase over time.
The data were entered by a lab assistant.
There is an exception to this rule: If you’re referring to an amount of data, in terms of size or storage, the singular form is used. For example: ‘The video data makes up 10GB.’

Can I write ‘the reason is because’ in academic writing?
Although ‘the reason is because’ has been used in the literature for centuries, there has been discussion about whether or not it is grammatically correct. There clearly is redundancy in the phrase, as ‘the reason is’ already indicates ‘because’. This is why many consider the phrase awkward or even incorrect.

While the phrase is common in conversation, you may want to avoid it in academic writing, and use ‘The reason is that’ or ‘because’ instead. For example: ‘These numbers are not reliable, because data points are missing.’

What’s a shorter version of ‘It should be taken into account that’?
‘It should be taken into account that’ is fine grammatically, but a bit lengthy. It can be shortened with a simple ‘Note that’. For example: ‘Note that these effects may disappear over time.’

Other constructions to use instead of ‘It should be taken into account that’ are:

It should be noted that
It should be mentioned that
It should be considered that
One should bear in mind that

What’s a shorter version of ‘due to the fact that’ or ‘based on the fact that’?
In many cases, both phrases can be replaced by ‘because’, ‘since’, or ‘as’. Note that this might slightly change the meaning. For example:

Production costs increased due to the fact that more apples were needed.The fact that more apples were needed directly increased production costs.
Production costs increased because / since more apples were needed.
Like ‘due to the fact that’, ‘because’ and ‘since’ indicate that the increased costs related directly to the fact that more apples were needed.

Production costs increased as more apples were needed.
The ‘as’ shows that the increase in apples went hand in hand with the increase in production cost. This can mean that the apples caused the increased production costs, but this isn’t explicit; maybe the two were just happening at the same time. If you add a comma after ‘increased’, there’s a stronger sense of cause and effect: ‘Production costs increased, as more apples were needed.’
